StoryWorlds

Overview

StoryWorlds is an open-source web-based system for collecting, storing, connecting, and presenting traditional knowledge in the form of stories, myths, and testimonies from multiple cultures and languages. The research team at the University of Pittsburgh School of Computing and Information, in partnership with academics in Ecuador, is working to pilot the application in the context of Ecuadorian Kichwa, with stories and associated media in Spanish and English.

Problem

As we began designing the initial iteration/proof of concept for StoryWorlds, several key challenges emerged: ensuring cultural accuracy and sensitivity, addressing language and accessibility barriers, and fostering community engagement and buy-in.

Approach

I conducted stakeholder interviews with 20+ academics across the United States as well as community members in Ecuador. Then, I used these perspectives to thoughtfully design a proof of concept for an interface that could effectively store stories for the Ecuadorian Quechuan community.

Preliminary Explorations

1. Evaluation of Existing Interface

My mentor, Dr. Dmitriy Babichenko, had created a bare-bones interface that incorporated the key ideas of using tags and colored nodes. However, challenges arose from overlapping tags, impacting the readability of content categorization and suggesting the need for improved usability in tag organization.

User flow representing all of the actions taken by a user during the onboarding process of the web or mobile app.

2. Comparison with Other Knowledge-Based Software

Analyzing the usability of other softwares that use a graph-based or knowledge-based format (Obsidian and Tinderbox) I was able to compare and contrast their features and consider what designs I wanted to implement for the StoryWorlds project. Obsidian's intuitive Markdown-based editor and graph-based visualization, as well as Tinderbox's unique spatial organization features, helped me identify best practices and refine my own interface design.

Obsidian Analysis:

Pros

  • Markdown-based editor for easy content creation and formatting.
  • Intuitive interface with simple, case-sensitive links (e.g., [[file]]) and backlinks.
  • Graph-based system for visualizing relationships.
  • Ability to group content into folders without disrupting the graph.
  • Obsidian canvas for spatial organization, with drag-and-drop features

Cons

  • Initial UX may feel overwhelming.
  • Graph adjustments can be confusing.
  • Hierarchical file/folder structure might not suit all scenarios.
  • Deleting nodes/files can be challenging.

Tinderbox Analysis:

Pros

  • Concept mapping with label connections (agree, disagree, clarify) and customization options (icons, colors, shapes).
  • Tangible interface resembling a virtual whiteboard for spatial organization.
  • Easy rearrangement of notes.

Cons

  • Less intuitive interface for some users, especially regarding link parking and certain functions.
  • Non-uniform size of icons can create visual clutter.
  • Default spread of nodes and font choices may seem less sophisticated.

User Interviews

I interviewed over a dozen academics in the fields of Anthropology, Indigenous Studies, Linguistics, Computer Science, Sociology, and Political Science over the course of May and June, gathering diverse perspectives that enriched the research process. Click here to access my interview questions.

Gathering their insights, I presented a summary of questions, concerns, and suggestions on six key themes to professors in Pittsburgh and Ecuador. These were some main themes:

Access

  • Digital divide (lack of internet) in remote communities
  • Search engine optimization
  • Impact of digital literacy on access

Localization

  • Localized images of ideas and concepts (What does "a tattoo" look like?)
  • Audio of words in associated languages
  • Impact of variations and dialects of language

Community Benefits

  • Realistic impact of project on community
  • Cultural preservation and analysis of folklore? Teaching device?
  • Danger of depicting cultural knowledge, practices, or stories is a way that community is representative

Collaboration

  • Diversity, equity, and inclusion
  • Storytellers need to be involved and compensated
  • Consider the long history of outside scholars drawing knowledge from indigenous communities without them having control over the representation

Development

  • Machine learning
  • Cross-cultural usability
  • Need to consider scope of project

Organization of Data

  • Difficulty of identifying data points when working with very rich oral narratives
  • What makes the graphical representation helpful?
  • What makes the graphical representation interesting?

Iterative Interface Design

New mockup of the concept map incorporating ideas from the interviews.

Wanting to address the specific concerns raised during interviews and incorporating valuable feedback from collaborators, I revised some screens of the mockup. This iterative process involved enhancing the user interface to ensure cultural sensitivity, improving the search functionality for easy accessibility, and implementing clickable elements for auditory learning experiences. Additionally, I created new screens with innovative features, such as localized image displays and interactive storytelling components, enhancing the overall usability and cultural relevance of the StorWworlds project.

Challenge

The primary challenge encountered during the study was related to user participation, specifically in engaging participants in scheduled Zoom calls. Given that many people in our survey database no longer volunteered at the same environmental organizations, it was challenging to incentivize their participation in the study effectively.
Some strategies we used to overcome this challenge include:

  • Offering non-monetary incentives (InvolveMINT Credits)
  • Offering flexible scheduling
  • Collaborating with student organizations and student-run community gardens
  • Maintaining regular communication and follow-ups
