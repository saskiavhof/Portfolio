StoryWorlds

UX/UI Research and UX/UI Design

Overview

StoryWorlds is an open-source web-based system for collecting, storing, connecting, and presenting traditional knowledge in the form of stories, myths, and testimonies from multiple cultures and languages. The research team at the University of Pittsburgh School of Computing and Information is working to pilot the application in the context of Ecuadorian Kichwa, with stories and associated media in Spanish and English.

Problem

An interface design for StoryWorlds that effectively stored and shared traditional knowledge and folklore needed to be created from scratch.

My mentor, Dr. Dmitriy Babichenko, had created a bare-bones interface that incorporated the key ideas of using tags and colored nodes. However, challenges arose from overlapping tags, impacting the readability of content categorization and suggesting the need for improved usability in tag organization.

2. Comparison with Other Knowledge-Based Software

Analyzing the usability of other softwares that use a graph-based or knowledge-based format (Obsidian and Tinderbox) I was able to compare and contrast their features and consider what designs I wanted to implement for the StoryWorlds project. Obsidian's intuitive Markdown-based editor and graph-based visualization, as well as Tinderbox's unique spatial organization features, helped me identify best practices and refine my own interface design.

Designing the Work Verification Feature

The high-fidelty screens my team designed using Figma.

Formerly called the "Proof of Impact", this feature of the mobile app allows users to choose a volunteer activity, specify where they want the credits to count towards, and log the completion of their work hours. This information then gets sent to a Community Partner, who verifies the work so that InvolveMINT can credit the ChangeMaker for their work.
The sample scenario we created for the mockup involved a ChangeMaker starting a "Clearing Japanese Knotweed" activity at Fairfield Community Garden.

A/B Testing

Video walking through the A/B testing of the work verification feature.

In this study, we aimed to investigate user preferences regarding two different credit verification methods for tracking progress: taking a before and after photo versus using a timer to clock in and out along with before and after photos. The study was conducted through two distinct tests, Test A and Test B, each employing different sequences of actions to assess user experiences and preferences.

Test A

  1. Users chose an activity.
  2. Users were prompted to take a before photo.
  3. Users clocked in to indicate the start of the activity.
  4. Users completed the activity.
  5. Users clocked out to indicate the end of the activity.
  6. Users submitted the credit to be verified.

Test B

  1. Users chose an activity.
  2. Users were prompted to take a before photo.
  3. Users completed the activity.
  4. Users took an after photo to document their progress.
  5. Users submitted the credit to be verified.

Challenge

The primary challenge encountered during the study was related to user participation, specifically in engaging participants in scheduled Zoom calls. Given that many people in our survey database no longer volunteered at the same environmental organizations, it was challenging to incentivize their participation in the study effectively.
Some strategies we used to overcome this challenge include:

  • Offering non-monetary incentives (InvolveMINT Credits)
  • Offering flexible scheduling
  • Collaborating with student organizations and student-run community gardens
  • Maintaining regular communication and follow-ups

Results

The A and B testing specifications involved two distinct approaches to credit verification tracking, with Test A incorporating a clock-in and out mechanism alongside before and after photos, and Test B focusing solely on photo documentation. Despite challenges related to user participation, the study's finding revealed a strong user preference for clock-in/out-based tracking, highlighting the appeal of monitoring the actual time spent on activities.
